# Karu — Business model

> Illustrative model for pitch purposes. Figures are assumptions to be validated with live marketplace data, not actuals. Benchmarks drawn from peer P2P rental marketplaces (Turo ~25% average take-rate; African P2P players NG-Ride, Muvment, Pull Up).

## How Karu makes money

Karu is a two-sided, verification-first rental marketplace. Revenue compounds as the platform aggregates supply (vendors) and demand (renters). Five lines, layered over time:

1. **Marketplace commission (take-rate)** — the core engine. A percentage of every booking, charged to the vendor (and/or split as a renter service fee). Target blended take-rate **15–20%**, below Turo's ~25% to win early supply, with room to expand as trust and demand deepen.
2. **Renter service fee** — a small, transparent booking fee on the renter side (illustrative **5–8%**), framed as the cost of verification and protection — the thing the informal market cannot offer.
3. **Verification & protection margin** — a per-trip protection/deposit-handling fee bundled into the booking. This is also the moat: it funds the checks that make "verified" real.
4. **Vendor subscription (tiered)** — optional monthly plans for professional vendors and small agencies (e.g. *Verified*, *Verified Pro*) offering priority placement, more listings, analytics, and faster payouts. Converts fragmented agencies into recurring revenue.
5. **Future / adjacent** — featured listings & promotion, insurance partnerships, MoMo/payment float, and data/lead products for fleet owners.

## Why this works in Cameroon specifically

- **Willingness to pay for trust is the whole thesis.** In a market where rental scams are a documented, named problem, verification is not a nice-to-have — it is the product. Renters will pay a modest fee to remove the gamble; vendors will pay to stand apart from the informal market.
- **Local rails lower friction and cost.** MoMo/FCFA collection avoids card penetration limits and FX friction that constrain global players.
- **Supply is cheap to acquire.** Thousands of idle private cars and fragmented agencies already exist; Karu monetises existing assets rather than buying a fleet (asset-light).

## Path to scale

- **Phase 1 — Liquidity (Douala + Yaoundé).** Win vendor supply with low/zero take-rate intro, prove trust operationally, build booking volume. *Currently pre-launch waitlist.*
- **Phase 2 — Monetise.** Introduce full take-rate + renter fee once liquidity holds; launch vendor subscriptions for agencies.
- **Phase 3 — Expand.** Additional cities (Bafoussam, Bamenda), adjacent revenue (protection, promotion), and defend against ride-hailing/super-app entry by owning the verified-rental category.

## The investability headline

Asset-light, proven model (works in Lagos, Nairobi, Johannesburg), **first to Cameroon**, monetising trust in a market where trust is the scarcest commodity — on local payment rails the global incumbents won't prioritise.
